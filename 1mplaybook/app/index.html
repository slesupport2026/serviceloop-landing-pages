Stage 1: Burden Rate. What It Costs to Keep the Doors Open

Your burden rate starts with one question: What does it cost to operate my business for one billable hour?

Step-by-Step:

1. Identify your total expenses. Pull from your accounting software, P&L sheet, or bookkeeping records. Include:

  • Your personal salary (this is a REQUIRED business expense. If you're not profitable on top of your base salary, everything else falls apart)
  • Investments and retirement savings (10% recommended)
  • A 90-day nest egg for stability
  • All business overhead and operations

2. Categorize your expenses:

A P&L statement always follows the same flow:

⚡ Formula

SALES REVENUE
(-) VARIABLE EXPENSES (COGS: Costs of Goods Sold)
= GROSS PROFIT
(-) FIXED EXPENSES (Overhead)
= NET PROFIT

Here's the simple test:

  • Does the expense increase with more work? It's a variable expense (COGS)
  • Does it decrease with less work? It's a variable expense (COGS)
  • Is it fixed no matter how much you work? It's a fixed expense (overhead)

Important: Materials are COGS, but we exclude materials from the burden rate. They get their own calculation later. Trust the process.

3. Define your billable units. Each active van/truck equals one unit. Only include what you currently have.

Key Warning: Many contractors project future vans or employees into their pricing model. This mistake lowers their rate and destroys profitability before those team members even exist. Price for what you have today.

4. Apply the 50% Rule.

You do not have 40 billable hours in a 40-hour week. Drive time. Materials runs. Callbacks. Setup. Training. Admin. That eats half your clock.

  • Full-time = 40 hours x 50 weeks = 2,000 hours/year
  • Apply the 50% rule: 1,000 billable hours per van, per year

Think of each unit not as a person, but as a working unit. A van and crew.

5. Calculate:

⚡ Formula

Total Variable Expenses + Total Fixed Expenses = Total Burden
Total Burden / Total Billable Hours = BURDEN RATE

Example: $236,000 in total expenses / 1,000 billable hours = $236/hr burden rate

Your burden rate should fall between $150 to $350/hr. This is your absolute basement. The cost of staying alive. You should RARELY use this number for anything other than reference.

If you looked at your number and felt a knot in your stomach, that's normal. Most electricians have never seen what it actually costs them to show up. That awareness alone is worth more than most business courses. But awareness without action is just anxiety. Let's keep building.

Stage 2: Growth Rate. What It Costs to Get Where You're Going

To build a rate that keeps you growing is critical. Electricians don't need to borrow money from banks or find investors. But you need to build growth into your rate or you'll be funding expansion out of personal sacrifice.

What the Growth Rate Covers:

  • New vans or vehicle deposits
  • Branding, wraps, and marketing investments
  • Team development and training (like this program)
  • Salary increases or performance incentives
  • Website upgrades and technology
  • Professional development and continued education

How to Calculate:

  • Look at your goals for the next 1 to 3 years
  • Estimate what these growth initiatives will cost
  • Divide by your total billable hours

Example: $90,000 in growth expenses / 1,000 billable hours = $90/hr growth rate

Your growth rate should fall between $50 to $150/hr.

"It is always better to be looking at the money than looking for it."

Pause here. Add your Burden Rate + Growth Rate. That combined number ($236 + $90 = $326/hr in our example) is your total rate BEFORE profit and materials.

If you kept working this hard for 12 months without changing this number... would anything actually change? That's the question most electricians never ask. And it's the reason they stay stuck.

The Profitable Rate. Your Non-Negotiable 30% Markup

Many contractors operate at or near break-even because they never intentionally build profit into pricing. Profit is not a bonus. It's a requirement. It funds reinvestment, financial security, unexpected expenses, and long-term stability.

The Calculation:

⚡ Formula

(Burden Rate + Growth Rate) x 0.30 = Markup
(Burden Rate + Growth Rate) + Markup = PROFITABLE RATE

Example:

  • Burden: $236/hr + Growth: $90/hr = $326/hr
  • 30% markup: $326 x 0.30 = $98/hr
  • Profitable Rate: $424/hr

Markup vs. Margin. Know the Difference.

A 30% markup does NOT equal a 30% margin. A 30% markup typically yields about 23% net profit once all operating costs are accounted for. This is intentional. It gives you real profit after the reality of running a business.

The Big Picture Math

If you work 1,000 billable hours/year and earn $100 profit/hour = $100,000 in net profit annually. That's what intentional pricing creates. That's what separates electricians who are busy from electricians who are building wealth.

Your profitable rate should fall between $250 to $500/hr.

There's a structure behind every consistent $80K+ month. And it starts right here, with this number.

Anything that would not exceed $50 in an hour of work: staples, romex, screws, boxes, plates, receptacles, switches, wire nuts, connectors, spackle.

Instead of wasting time building a work order BEFORE they sell (estimating every part or relying on pricebooks), this method embraces the "Sales First" methodology. Sell it, then build it.

The Estimation Formula. Pricing Any Job in Minutes.

Once you have your SSR, pricing any job is simple:

⚡ Formula

(Estimated Hours x SSR) + Specialty Materials = Job Price

  • Consumable materials (romex, boxes, receptacles) are already baked into your SSR at $50/hr
  • Specialty materials (panels, generators, anything beyond consumables) get added on top
  • Third-party services (permits, inspections, subcontractors) get added on top
  • Memberships (if offered as part of the option) get added on top

Example: Panel replacement, 10 hours:

  • Labor: 10 x $424/hr = $4,240
  • Consumable materials: 10 x $50/hr = $500 (already in your SSR)
  • Specialty materials (arc fault breakers, surge protectors, permits): quoted separately
  • Base price before specialty: $4,740

That's it. No 1,000-item pricebook. No fumbling through catalogs. No estimating every screw.

The 9 Truths of Marketing

Before we talk tactics, you need to internalize these truths. Think of them as a compass that brings you back to what works whenever you feel lost or overwhelmed.

1. Be the One with the Megaphone

If you don't tell the world who you are and what you do, no one else will. The loud and consistent voices win. Confidence is not arrogance. It's service with conviction. Get on the hill and let people know you exist.

2. Message and Frequency Are Everything

It's not just what you say, but how often you say it. Use the language your customers understand, not technical jargon. Speak about what your service does for them, not what it's called. Repeat your message often so it becomes familiar and trusted.

3. You Are Renting, Not Owning

You don't own Facebook, Google, or any social media platform. They can change overnight. Use them to attract attention, but always bring leads into your own system. Email lists, text updates, newsletters. Own the relationship, not just the attention.

4. Your Service IS Your Marketing

Every conversation, every follow-up, every thank-you message is marketing. Stay in touch with past clients through regular communication. Be human, be consistent, and remind them you're there to help.

5. Leads Have a Definite Cost

There is no such thing as a free lead. Every lead costs either time, money, or both. When you recognize that, you stop treating marketing as an expense and start treating it as an investment.

6. Don't Let Marketing Burn You Out

Marketing can feel like a full-time job, but you're not meant to do it alone. Build systems and hire help as you grow.

7. Repetition Builds Recognition

Don't be afraid to repeat yourself. People rarely see your message the first time. Say it again and again until your audience can repeat it for you. That's when you know it's stuck.

8. Marketing Is a Skill You Practice

Just like electrical work, marketing takes repetition and refinement. Plan, Do, Check, Act. Test ideas, study results, adjust, improve.

9. More Beats Less

Consistency always wins. Your business runs in waves: leads, sales, installs. If you stop marketing, those waves crash.

No Options = Price War. Every Time.

If you offer a single option, you're not offering a choice at all. There are only two possible outcomes: the customer accepts it or walks away. And when they walk away, they walk straight to the next guy's quote. You just trained your customer to compare you.

You didn't lose on price. You lost on clarity. Your offer was invisible, so price became the only thing they could see.

"If price was everything, luxury wouldn't exist. Customers don't buy the lowest price. They buy the clearest value."

When you provide multiple options, something shifts. Instead of focusing on whether they want to buy, they begin deciding how they want to buy. The conversation changes from "Is this expensive?" to "Which option fits my home best?"

You're not bidding anymore. You're advising. And advisors don't get undercut.

What to expect from this chapter: Building your first 6-option set will take about 45 minutes. It will feel awkward. Your instinct will be to offer 3 and call it done. Push through. By your third or fourth option set, you'll be building them in 15 minutes. By your tenth, it's muscle memory. The partner who went from $675 to $6,011 on his first 3 presentations? He built his options the night before. Imperfectly. And it still 9x'd his revenue.

"My customers won't sit through 6 options." They won't sit through you reading 6 options. That's true. You present the top option, explain it briefly, then hand the tablet over and let them look. They choose. You don't read. Joseph covers this in Secret 7 of Chapter 5. But the short version: customers LOVE having choices. What they hate is being lectured. Six options with silence beats one option with a 20-minute pitch every single time.

Why Three Options Aren't Enough

In a three-option system ("Good, Better, Best"), each buyer type only gets one true selection. It's still an ultimatum dressed up as choice.

The Six-Option Model

  • 2 Premium options (Platinum, Gold)
  • 2 Mid-range options (Silver, Bronze)
  • 2 Economy options (Economy, Basic)

Why six? Because it prevents "take it or leave it" pressure. Covers every buyer type without prejudging. Keeps the technician out of the way of the sale. Reduces the need for customers to seek other estimates. And provides the perfect balance: enough variety to feel empowered, not so many they're overwhelmed.

Most electricians present one or two options and wonder why they get price-shopped. The six-option model raises your conversion rate AND your average ticket at the same time. Those two things aren't supposed to move together. But they do, because you've changed the entire psychology of the conversation.

Profit First. The 4 Pillars of Service Finance.

1. Pricing (You've Done This)

2. Cashflow. The Weekly Rhythm.

Cash is oxygen. Revenue on paper means nothing if you can't make payroll.

The Cash Bucket System:

  • Operating bucket. Covers weekly expenses (payroll, materials, fuel).
  • Tax bucket. Set aside 25 to 30% of profit for taxes before you touch it.
  • Profit bucket. Your personal reward. Take it monthly. Don't reinvest all of it.
  • Growth bucket. Funded by your Growth Rate (Chapter 1). Used only for planned expansion.

Weekly Cashflow Rhythm:

  • Monday: Review last week's collected revenue
  • Allocate to buckets (operating, tax, profit, growth)
  • Review outstanding invoices and follow up
  • Check: Can I make payroll? Can I cover this week's materials?
  • If any bucket is low, that's a signal, not a crisis. Adjust before it becomes one.

Key Metrics:

  • Days Sales Outstanding (DSO): how fast you collect
  • Cash Conversion Cycle: how fast revenue becomes usable cash
  • Weekly collection rate: target 90%+ of invoiced work collected within 7 days

Most electricians look at their bank balance and feel rich or panicked based on a single number. The bucket system turns that anxiety into clarity. You'll know exactly where you stand every Monday morning. And you'll make decisions from data, not fear.

3. The Five Profit Levers

Lever 1: Revenue per Van. How much does each unit produce? Benchmark: 1,000 hours x your SSR.

Lever 2: Material Cost %. Should be 10 to 15% of revenue. If you're above 15%, you're leaking. Track monthly.

Lever 3: Labor Efficiency. Billable hours as % of total hours. Target: 50%+. Every 5% improvement recovers tens of thousands.

Lever 4: Overhead Discipline. Fixed costs as % of revenue. As you grow, this % should shrink. If it doesn't, you're scaling your problems.

Lever 5: Average Ticket. The compound effect. A 10% improvement in ticket, with the same call volume and close rate, is a 10% revenue boost with zero additional cost.

What Are You Building This For?

There comes a moment in every electrical business where the question changes. It's no longer "How do I grow?" It's "What am I building this for?"

Growth and scale are not the same thing.

Scale means more revenue without proportionally more cost, time, or stress. Systems run without you. Revenue grows faster than headcount. You're the architect, not the electrician.

Exit means building something someone else would pay to own. Valuation is based on profit, systems, and transferability. Not on you showing up every day.
